From Bank Vaults to Agentic Crews

BUILDING AI THAT ACTS, NOT JUST ANSWERS · 13 MIN READ · KEERTHAN NEKKANTI

For three years at Bank of America I shipped neural networks that watched 10 million transactions a week, NLP models that hunted money laundering patterns, and a synthetic data engine that let the world's compliance teams build without ever touching production. Then I started MapShock, an agentic AI platform with 46 specialists. The lesson, in both: a great pirate captain doesn't do everything — they assemble a crew.

The shape of an agentic system

The simplest way I can describe an agentic AI system to a non-engineer is this:

A regular AI answers. An agentic AI does. It plans, calls tools, reads what comes back, decides what to do next, and only stops when the job is finished — or when the captain says stop.

Concretely, every serious agentic system I've shipped or studied has the same five layers, regardless of stack:

1 · Captain
Top-level orchestrator. Decomposes goal into tasks.
2 · Crew of specialists
Domain agents. Finance, geopolitics, supply chain, etc.
3 · Tools
APIs, search, code execution, databases, sensors.
4 · Memory & state
What was tried, what was learned, what was cited.
5 · Quality gates
Verification, evaluation, human-in-the-loop checkpoints.

What banking taught me about agentic systems

Lesson 1 — Real systems live and die by their controls

At BofA the system that mattered most wasn't the model itself — it was the quality framework around it. We absorbed a 60% volume surge with zero downtime not because the algorithm was magical, but because the data quality controls, retries, and observability layers were treated as first-class engineering. The same is true for agents.

An agent that hallucinates a citation in a research workflow is the same kind of failure as a pipeline that silently drops a million sanctions records. The fix is not "a better model." The fix is structured verification. That is exactly why I baked a 6-gate quality pipeline into MapShock — every analytical claim from any of the 46 agents has to come back with cited, timestamped evidence, or the captain rejects it.

Lesson 2 — Specialization beats generality, until orchestration shows up

One huge model can do a hundred jobs poorly. A hundred small specialists can do them well, but a hundred specialists with no captain is chaos. The hardest engineering in agentic systems isn't building the agents — it's routing, state management, and conflict resolution when two agents disagree.

This is exactly the fraud-detection problem I worked on in a different costume. Multiple models, each looking at part of the picture; one orchestrator deciding what to escalate, what to dismiss, and how to explain the call.

Lesson 3 — Synthetic data is the cheat code

One of the proudest things I shipped at BofA was a synthetic data generation engine — a model that produced compliant fake data so global teams could build apps without ever touching protected production records. The same pattern is becoming critical for agentic AI:

  • You cannot test an agent at scale on real users without serious risk.
  • Synthetic environments — simulated markets, synthetic case files, fake user journeys — let you stress-test before anyone gets hurt.
  • Synthetic data is also how you fairly evaluate agents on rare-but-critical scenarios that real data underrepresents.

Why I built MapShock

MapShock came out of a simple frustration. Strategic intelligence work — the kind of thing consulting firms charge huge fees for — is mostly an orchestration problem. You need a researcher, a finance analyst, a geopolitics expert, a cyber analyst, a supply-chain person, and an editor, all coordinated, all citing their sources, all hitting deadline.

Modern LLMs can play any of those roles individually. The interesting question is whether they can play them together — like a Straw Hat crew, each member doing what they're good at, captain holding the wheel.

How the platform is structured

  • 46 specialized agents — each tuned to a domain (macro finance, geopolitics, cybersecurity, supply chain, regulatory) with its own retrieval surface and prompting strategy.
  • Protocol-driven orchestrator — instead of one giant prompt, the orchestrator runs a deterministic protocol that decides which agents to call, in what order, with what context.
  • 6-gate quality pipeline — every output is checked for source attribution, recency, internal consistency, refutability, factual grounding, and final coherence. Anything that fails goes back for revision.
  • Cited & timestamped evidence — there is no "trust me" output. Every claim points to a source with a date.

The mistakes I see most people make

  • Building agents before defining the workflow. If you cannot draw the workflow as a flowchart, do not give it to an agent. The agent will just hide your missing process behind plausible language.
  • Ignoring evaluation until the demo works. A demo that works once is not a system. You need an evaluation harness before you scale.
  • Confusing "the model called the API" with "the agent finished the job." Tool use is the easy part. Knowing when the job is actually done is the hard part.
  • Treating safety as a moderation filter. True safety in agentic systems comes from constraints, scopes, sandboxes, and structured verification — not just a refusal classifier.
The Straw Hats almost never won by being individually the strongest. They won because every member fit a slot and the captain trusted them with it. Agentic AI is the same engineering discipline, written in code.

What the next two years probably look like

  1. Agents go vertical. Generic "do anything" agents will lose to deeply specialized agents wired into specific workflows: legal review, financial diligence, code review, compliance, customer ops.
  2. Evaluation becomes a profession. The most valuable engineers in AI shops over the next two years won't be model builders — they'll be the people who design honest evaluations.
  3. Memory becomes the product. The agent that remembers your business correctly across sessions is far more valuable than the one that's smart for ten minutes and forgets.
  4. Quality gates go from optional to default. Anything that ships into a regulated context will require structured verification by construction. The 6-gate idea will become normal infrastructure.

What I bring to this fight
