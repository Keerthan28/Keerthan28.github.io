The Search for AGI

HUNTING THE ALL BLUE · 12 MIN READ · KEERTHAN NEKKANTI

In One Piece, every pirate has heard of the All Blue — a sea where every fish in the world swims together. It is myth, it is destination, and it is the reason crews put up sails. AGI is our All Blue: a single intelligence that meets every problem the human mind throws at it, in any modality, on any scale.

What people actually mean by AGI

Most arguments about Artificial General Intelligence collapse because nobody agrees on the goalposts. So let me name three useful definitions a working engineer can hold in one head:

  1. Economic AGI — a system that can do most knowledge-economy work as well as a competent human. Measurable. Boring. Plausible within a decade.
  2. Cognitive AGI — a system that learns any new skill from a small number of examples, transfers across modalities, and reasons about its own reasoning. Harder. Probably the real prize.
  3. Metaphysical AGI — a system with selfhood, intentions, and inner life. Mostly a religion, mostly not engineering.

If you're shipping today, you're building toward the first one. If you're researching, you're climbing toward the second. The third is what people fight about on Twitter.

Why the current Grand Line is not yet the All Blue

Large language models are extraordinary tools, but the gap between fluent and general is the entire ocean. Three honest limits I keep running into when shipping AI systems at work and in projects like MapShock:

1. They are sample-hungry, not sample-efficient

A child learns "this is a duck" from one drawing. A frontier model learns it from internet-scale exposure. The Devil Fruit power of few-shot abstraction — actually inferring a rule from a tiny set — is still mostly cosplay over interpolation.

2. They reason in vibes, not invariants

Ask a model the same question with different framing and you can get different answers. A general intelligence should hold an invariant — a stable belief that is robust to the dressing of the prompt. Right now we get plausible essays, not invariants.

3. They have no body, no clock, no stake

Intelligence in the wild is shaped by feedback from a world with consequences. Today's models are trained in a sealed library, then deployed into a world they can describe but not feel. Closing this loop — robotics, sensors, persistent memory, real outcomes — is what makes the next generation of agentic systems different from chatbots.

The Going Merry didn't become the Thousand Sunny because the crew added more sails. It happened because Franky built a new kind of ship. AGI will be the same — it won't come from one more zero of compute, it will come from a new architecture.

The architectures most likely to become the Thousand Sunny

If I had to bet on what carries us from "very smart text" to "actually general," I'd watch four bets in parallel:

  • Agentic systems with tools and memory — exactly the territory MapShock plays in. Not one giant model that does everything, but a captain model that orchestrates 40+ specialist agents, calling tools, keeping state, and citing its sources. This is also closest to how human teams already work.
  • World models trained from video and simulation — once a model can predict what happens next in a frame of reality, you have a learning substrate that goes way beyond text.
  • Self-improvement loops — models that propose, verify, and refine their own training data, especially for code, math, and other formally checkable domains.
  • Neuromorphic and energy-efficient compute — biological brains run on ~20 watts. The path from 20 megawatts back down to 20 watts is going to bend the entire economics of AGI.

Why governance is part of the build, not an afterthought

One Piece's world also has Cipher Pol, Marine HQ, and the World Government. Every powerful technology eventually gets one. AGI will, too — and the engineers who treat governance, evaluation, and safety as part of the engineering surface will be the ones building things that ship inside real institutions.

At BofA I learned that systems handling real money or real risk live or die by their controls. AGI is no different. The 6-gate quality pipeline I built into MapShock — verifying every analytical claim is backed by cited, timestamped evidence — is a small version of what serious AGI systems will need at the population scale.

What I'm betting on

Three convictions I act on in my own work:

  • Generality emerges from orchestration before it emerges from a single model. Build crews of agents now; the captain model will get smarter over time.
  • The bottleneck is evaluation, not capability. We can already build systems that look general; the hard problem is knowing when they actually are.
  • The All Blue is real, but it is reached one storm at a time. No single breakthrough will get us there. A thousand small ones will.

Until then — keep tightening the rigging, keep the log pose calibrated, and keep sailing.
