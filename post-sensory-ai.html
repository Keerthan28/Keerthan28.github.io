Sensory Devices & AI

ENGINEERING OBSERVATION HAKI · 11 MIN READ · KEERTHAN NEKKANTI

In One Piece, the strongest fighters do not see with their eyes alone. Observation Haki — Kenbunshoku — lets them feel a presence around a corner, dodge a bullet by reading intent, and predict the next swing of a sword. Modern AI, paired with sensors, is quietly trying to grow the same sense.

The three Hakis, mapped to AI

Once you start looking, the parallel between Haki and modern AI systems becomes uncannily clean:

Observation · Kenbunshoku
Sensing the world — multimodal perception, IoT, anomaly detection
Armament · Busoshoku
Acting on the world — actuators, robotics, agentic execution
Conqueror · Haoshoku
Coordinating systems — orchestration, swarm intelligence, market-shaping models

This essay is mostly about the first one — the sensory layer — because that's where the next decade of useful AI is going to be born.

Why sensors are eating AI

The big language models you've heard of were trained on the largest written record in human history. That well isn't bottomless. The next 10x of training data is not more text — it's the world itself, captured in sensors. Cameras, microphones, radar, lidar, thermal, IMUs, biosignals, satellite spectra, soil moisture probes, kitchen scales.

Three forces are bending compute toward the sensor edge:

  1. Cheap silicon. A $3 microcontroller now fits a tiny neural net for keyword spotting, fall detection, or arrhythmia classification.
  2. Foundation models for perception. CLIP-style and segment-anything-style models give us reusable "eyes" that no single team has to retrain from scratch.
  3. Energy ceilings. You cannot send every camera frame to a hyperscale GPU. The world will run on tiered intelligence: fast, dumb sensing locally; slow, smart reasoning in the cloud.

Five places where sensors + AI are already changing the rules

1. Healthcare on the wrist

A modern smartwatch is a six-sensor device pretending to be a watch. Optical heart rate, ECG, SpO₂, accelerometer, temperature, and now glucose-trend estimators. Pair these with on-device models and you get arrhythmia alerts, sleep staging, and early-warning systems for conditions that used to require a full clinic visit.

2. Cars learning to feel

Self-driving systems are essentially a giant exercise in fusing bad sensors into one good belief. Cameras lie when sun-blinded, lidar lies in snow, radar lies on flat metal. The interesting research isn't "which sensor wins" — it's how to fuse contradictory streams into a single trustworthy world model. That fusion problem is general.

3. Industrial and environmental telemetry

Bridges, dams, transformers, pipelines, soil — there are millions of expensive things in the world that fail without warning, and that we now wrap in cheap sensors. The challenge is no longer collecting data, it's separating signal from noise at scale and acting on it before failure. This is straight out of my BofA playbook: 100M+ records, real-time pipelines, anomaly detection.

4. Multimodal personal AI

The interesting frontier is not the chat box — it's the assistant that sees what you see. AR glasses, in-ear models, ambient room mics. The privacy questions here are gigantic and the engineering is harder than people think. Latency, on-device compute, and edge-to-cloud handoff are the new bottlenecks.

5. Security at the edge

This is where my IEEE work on lightweight ciphers comes back. When sensors are tiny and power-starved, you cannot run the heavyweight crypto your laptop uses. You need ciphers like SIT, LEA, Twine, and PRESENT that produce real security at single-watt budgets. Without that, the entire sensory AI stack is one packet capture away from being weaponized against the people it serves.

An AI without sensors is a sage in a sealed library. An AI with sensors is Zoro the moment Mihawk finally teaches him to listen. Same brain, different sea.

The hard problems nobody talks enough about

  • Sensor calibration drift. A model trained on Sensor Generation N quietly degrades when the manufacturer ships Generation N+1. Continual learning at scale is mostly an unsolved engineering discipline.
  • Multimodal alignment is harder than multimodal demos. A model that can name what it sees is not the same as a model that can act on what it sees with the right priorities.
  • Privacy isn't a feature; it's an architecture. If sensitive sensor data ever leaves the device unencrypted, the system is already broken. End-to-end encryption, federated learning, and on-device inference are no longer optional.
  • Energy budgets are the new latency budgets. Whoever wins the next decade of edge AI will be the team that does the most useful inference per watt-hour, not per millisecond.

What I'm building toward

Two convictions are guiding what I work on next:

  • The most useful AI is multimodal, on-device, and cheap. The future is a thousand small models living inside the things that already touch the world, coordinated by a smaller number of cloud captains.
  • Security is the spine of sensory AI. Without it, the sensors become the attack surface. The work I started on lightweight ciphers and the controls I built into compliance pipelines at BofA both point at the same lesson: you must design for adversaries from line one.

Observation Haki, in the end, is not magic. It's training. It's pattern recognition built from a lifetime of noticing the small things. AI plus sensors is doing the same thing — building a generation of systems that notice what we don't, fast enough to matter.
